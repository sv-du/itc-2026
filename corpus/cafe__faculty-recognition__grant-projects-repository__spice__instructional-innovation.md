### Kent Dickson

### Orientalist visions and colonizing forces: enforced modernity in the global south

×

**Applicant:**
Kent Dickson

**Collaborators:**
Hyeryung Hwang (IGE)

**Amount Funded:**
$5,140.00

**Proposal Abstract:**This proposal requests funds to develop a multi-disciplinary instructional module and digital gallery that will be used in several IGE and Spanish courses–IGE 1100, IGE 1200, SPN 3420, and SPN 4320, in particular. The learning module and gallery will be based on antique postcards from the 1900-1920 period in Korea and Mexico. In addition to the digital gallery, the collaborators from IGE and Spanish will develop a physical display that can be used in in-person classes. This project is to be funded by summer Special Consultant Appointments (1 WTU for each faculty member) with work to be carried out in Summer 2022, to be implemented in Fall 2022. The project aligns well with the goal of creating multi-format course materials to be used in a variety of instructional modalities. Likewise, by concentrating on Mexican and Korean source material and contexts, it emphasizes inclusive engagement and equity-minded strategies that will speak to Latinx and Asian American students. We have designed this project as a comparative, multicultural and active-learning experience, closely aligned with CPP’s Signature Polytechnic Identity, and have proposed it for the PolyX Hub.

## Societal responses to climate change

### Gabriel Granco

### Societal responses to climate change

×

**Applicant:**
Gabriel Granco

**Collaborators:**John Korah

**Amount Funded:**
$12,600.00

**Proposal Abstract:**Climate change is the key global challenge that has the potential to impact all aspects of human activity. Computational modeling and simulation techniques have emerged as the primary tools for accessing and predicting the impacts of climate change. Climate change sciences also deal with vast amounts of data, making this suitable for Big Data analytics. New advances in computational and statistical techniques have also helped researchers tackle challenging problems in this arena. However, the pedagogy and undergraduate level curriculum have not kept pace with the advances in the field. In this project, we seek to create new curriculum that will bridge the gaps in climate change science pedagogy as it relates to computational modeling and simulation, and the need to include realistic representations of human and social behaviors within these models. CPP students are experiencing society’s response to climate change as Los Angeles and Southern California face more climate related events. The culturally responsive project-based module will take four weeks in which students will explore one issue that they observe in their community using geospatial data analysis of new socio-ecological datasets. In the project-based module, the students will activate several components of Bloom’s taxonomy, developing competence to make observations of their community, to summarize and apply new methods, and to analyze their community response to climate change using advanced computational methods. Additionally, we are expecting to create a pathway for non-Computer Science majors to be trained in advanced computational modeling and simulation techniques and for Computer Science and related majors to access to this interdisciplinary topic. This SPICE  grant will fund the development of a four-week module to be applied to three classes, GEO 3070/L – Climate Change, GEO 4450/L – Environmental Modelling with GIS, and CS 4990 – Undergraduate Special Topics in Social Computing. It will also support the preparation of an external grant application to the NSF IUSE Level-I proposal that will extend this module into a Course-based Undergraduate Research Experience (CURE) to be cross-listed between Geography and Computer Science, as well as in the GIS Minor and Data Analysis Minor.

## Transitioning to R: instructor and student manuals for research methods

### Robert Nyenhuis

### Transitioning to R: instructor and student manuals for research methods

×

**Applicant:**
Robert Nyenhuis

**Collaborators:**Mario Guerrero, Neil Chaturvedi

**Amount Funded:**
$12,600.00

**Proposal Abstract:**Over the last few years, the Political Science department has revised its research methods sequence, spurred by discussions from our Faculty Methods sub-committee. Specifically, we have added a methods sequence, encompassing two semesters: Introduction to Research Methods (PLS 2051) and Advanced Research Methods (PLS 3055). Previously, we required only one methods course; however, it has now been split into two to allow students to focus on basic issues of research design prior to learning statistical analysis. The change was partially motivated by the transformation of job opportunities for our majors and preparing our students for the future of work. As such, we wish to examine what effect this sequencing has had on student learning outcomes and our department’s broader course learning outcomes.

More recently, we have identified a glaring weakness in our instructional modality – that we provide instruction to students using the SPSS software. Given the virtual nature of much of our instruction since the COVID pandemic began, this presents significant logistical issues. Specific and central to criterion #2 of the SPICE grant application (improvement in flexible learning modalities), we seek funding to revamp and revitalize our department’s research methods sequence by transitioning our instruction of research methods using the free and publicly available R software. At present, students access SPSS using the university’s virtual lab. However, once students graduate, access to the SPSS software program ends. Currently, a SPSS single-user license costs $1,200 per year. Shifting our instruction to learning R will allow our students to continue using the software package well beyond their years at Cal Poly Pomona, and at no cost.

## Creating a model for 'study away' programs in the midst of covid

### Renford Reese

### Creating a model for 'study away' programs in the midst of covid

×

**Applicant:**
Renford Reese

**Amount Funded:**
$16,500.00

**Proposal Abstract:**The author has taken 17 groups of Cal Poly Pomona students on Study Abroad trips to 10 countries over the past two decades. The Study Abroad experience, for those who have participated, is consistently one of the most memorable highlights of their college experience. The COVID-19 pandemic has all but stopped short-term Study Abroad programming. This pandemic has denied students who have dreamed of going to international destinations to learn and grow. Given this new reality, the author suggests that we temporarily replace the "Study Abroad" model with a "Study Away" model. The fundamental dynamics of the Study Away model is the same as the Study Abroad model. The idea is to take students to local, regional, or national destinations that give them a different, dynamic, and experiential learning outlet. The author teaches an "NGO & Social Service Outreach" course during the Fall Semester in which students create their own nonprofit organizations. The culminating activity for this course is to do a week-long internship with an NGO in an international destination. These NGO internship trips have taken place in Amsterdam, Vancouver, London, and Mumbai over the past decade. The COVID-19 pandemic compelled the author to search for regional destinations that were within driving range. This strategy was to avoid the uncertainty of air travel. During the Winter Intersession 2022, the author took 22 Cal Poly Pomona students to Palm Desert, California to do week-long internships in five impactful NGOs in the area: FIND Food Bank, The Living Desert Zoo & Botanical Gardens, CREATE Center for the Arts, Friends of the Desert Mountains, and the Family YMCA of the Desert. Students selected which organization they wanted to intern with. The Palm Desert NGO Internship trip was just as empowering, inspirational, transformative, and meaningful as the author's Study Abroad programs had been. This trip inspired the author to apply for this SPICE grant to create a Study Away model for other Cal Poly Pomona faculty to embrace.

## How to create a podcast: developing a research dissemination and public engagement with science course

### Amy Dao

### How to create a podcast: developing a research dissemination and public engagement with science course

×

**Applicant:**
Amy Dao

**Amount Funded:**
$22,380.00

**Proposal Abstract:**
The SPICE Instructional Innovation grant will support the development of a course on Research Dissemination and Public Engagement with Science that will be taught in Spring 2023. The aim of the course is to teach students how to effectively communicate their research insights to the general public through digital storytelling and podcasting. Although the explosion of digital media has facilitated more avenues for widespread dissemination of research, traditional outputs such as journal articles, books, and conference presentations continue to be the primary vehicle for communicating research. While valuable, these avenues often limit knowledge sharing. Although many view wider dissemination and public engagement with science to be fundamental to open and effective science, students in the social sciences are rarely given formal training on how to communicate their findings to non-specialists. This SPICE grant aims to correct this problem by training the next generation of undergraduate researchers in impactful research dissemination techniques through a partnership with StoryCenter—a non-profit organization that has trained over 15,000 people in digital storytelling and podcasting. The skills acquired in the course will prepare students for long term careers in research, scientific communication, and/or graduate school. Students will learn the art and practice of informing, educating, and raising awareness of science-related topics.

How we communicate research is changing because of new possibilities enabled by digital technology. Stories are not idle tales or imaginings; rather they are hardworking cultural forces intimately connected to how we engage with the world and what we believe is possible. The course will use the power of storytelling to humanize pandemic experiences and train students while disseminating study results for posterity.

## Developing a digital library of educational content to maintain the prerequisite relations among courses in industrial and manufacturing engineering program curriculum

### Saeideh Fallah Fini

### Developing a digital library of educational content to maintain the prerequisite relations among courses in industrial and manufacturing engineering program curriculum

×

**Applicant:**
Saeideh Fallah Fini

**Amount Funded:**
$4,200.00

**Proposal Abstract:**The subject of Probability and Statistical Analysis, taught in IME3140 course, is a critical prerequisite to five core classes in Industrial and Manufacturing Engineering (IME) program curriculum. However, there is usually a gap of at least one (up to three) semester between taking the Probability and Statistical Analysis course and proceeding core IME courses. Thus, majority of students forget the probability and statistics knowledge they are expected to retain and use as they get to their core IME classes. This undesirable situation does significantly hurt the performances of students in the proceeding core IME classes. The goal of this proposal is to enable students to easily refresh their memory with respect to prerequisite probability and statistics knowledge they are expected to know/remember as they take their core IME classes by developing a “prerequisite probability and statistics knowledge” module for each one of five core IME course that requires such knowledge as prerequisite, total of 5 such modules. These modules will serve as refresher for probability and statistics concepts students need to know as they take each one of core IME classes. The five “prerequisite probability and statistics knowledge” modules will form a digital library of educational content that will be shared with all IME faculties through Canvas Common. The instructor(s) of each core IME course can find the module designed for that specific core course in Canvas Common, import it into their course Canvas page, and share it with their students. Students can review the content of the module on their own time/speed and take the assigned quiz by a deadline specified by the instructor (preferably in the first two weeks of the semester). The success of the proposed modules in helping students refresh their memory with respect to probability and statistics knowledge and successfully using that in the proceeding courses will be assessed by monitoring performance of students in each one of five core IME classes through changes in percentage of D, F, & W grades over time.

## Unveiling the history of physics with a lens of inclusivity & equality

### Homeyra Sadaghiani

### Unveiling The History Of Physics With a Lens Of Inclusivity & Equality

×

**Applicant:**
Homeyra Sadaghiani

**Amount Funded:**
$6,900.00

**Proposal Abstract:**The story of physics is often told through the lens of our culture and human biases. Physics textbooks highlight the accomplishments of merely a few scientists whom we credit with equations, laws, or theories in their names alone. What often goes unmentioned is the fact that neither of these men worked alone; each benefited from a rich network of collaborators and each stoop on the shoulders of hardworking individuals who came before them. Forgetting this leads to a problem: most of us begin to believe that the small cohort of individual geniuses is responsible for the sciences’ great deeds and discoveries. Additionally, those who are named in history often were privileged, had a higher social status, were more resources, dominated the society and culture in some form- or merely were just lucky. Ironically, a large majority of these scientists and nearly all the physicists named in our history are white European males. However, we all know that is not the whole story; the story of science is the story of humanity and the contributions of a wide range of diverse populations all over the world have brought us to where we are today.

I plan to substantially redesign a GE area D course (PHY 3060- History of Physics) to be more inclusive and equitable. I also plan to make this course to be a flexible high-quality hybrid course by providing a range of accessible online materials, inviting expert guest speakers to classrooms, and giving students choices on their assignments as well as opportunities to present their own research findings on the unrecognized physicists. This course will enhance our students’ social awareness about the biases that have influenced the history of science. First, incorporating the often-forgotten stories of underrepresented groups in the physics classroom can be an effective strategy to communicate a more inclusive narrative of the discipline. Second, setting these discoveries within students’ local historical context reinforces the fact that modern scientific progress is largely attributed to successful cooperation within and across scientific collaborations.

This project is deliberately designed at its core to incorporate inclusive strategies leading to equitable outcomes for all students. The redesign process aims to improve and enhance teaching and learning in flexible learning modalities, including proven effective pedagogical activities to improve student learning outcomes, perspective, and attitudes, measured with validated and assessment tools. Further, the budget and expenses are reasonable and well justified. The project cost is reasonable, justified, a one-time expenditure, with no ongoing expenses, making it easily sustainable.

### Ericsson Marin

### Cyber Defender Pathway (CDP): a PolyX program for cybersecurity instruction

×

**Applicant:**
Ericsson Marin

**Collaborators:**
Tingting Chen, Abdelfattha Amamra, and Mohammad Husain

**Amount Funded:**
$22,800.00

**Proposal Abstract:**
U.S. organizations, including the National Security Agency (NSA), the Department of Homeland Security (DHS), Department of Defense (DoD), Government Accountability Office (GAO), and the Bureau of Labor Statistics have identified a substantial shortage in cyber defense, and the scenario tends to get worse. There were 299,000 active openings in August 2017 across the private sector and the government, and the estimate is 1.8 million unfilled positions by 2022. To strengthen the cybersecurity workforce around U.S., this proposal outlines a PolyX program for cybersecurity instruction named “Cyber Defender Pathway (CDP).” CDP will serve Computer Science (CS) and related majors (CIS and ECE) and will provide activities to help students meet their career aspirations in cyber. To enable its full implementation, an online management platform will be designed and developed to manage student accomplishments throughout the program, with the issuance of state-of-the art security digital badges for those who complete all CDP requirements.
