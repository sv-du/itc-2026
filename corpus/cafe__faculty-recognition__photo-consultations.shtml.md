# Faculty Consultation Method Based on Still Photography

April Dawn, former university photographer, and CAFE Lead, Senior Instructional Designer, and Faculty Development Consultant, offered faculty consultations using black-and-white photographic essays to enhance classroom experiences. This service provided educators with visual insights into their teaching environments, with a focus on student interactions and classroom dynamics.

![](../img/photo-consultations/joanne-sohn.jpg)

## Joanne Sohn - Spring 2024

**AHS 4407L** *Critical Care, Advanced Surgical Assisting and Anesthesiology Lab***AHS 4408L** *Surgical Recovery, Nursing and Critical Care Lab*Don B. Huntley College of Agriculture

faculty reflection

### Joanne Sohn

×

"I’ve shared these photos with my students, colleagues and friends… and they were amazed! These photos not only show what we do but also how the students feel about their experience. April Dawn spent hours in our class, taking thousands of pictures. Her endurance was amazing as it was a six-hour surgery lab. We have real patients, and the students know that it is a high-stakes situation. The students, however, usually say it is their favorite class at CPP. Through these photographs, we can see not only their skills at work, but how they are learning these skills. It was especially rewarding for me to see how engaged the students were during class. As it is a stressful, fast-paced class with multiple groups, I am not always able to see how students are receiving the information. The pictures allowed me to see how to improve my teaching and let others see how much the students have learned in our program. Thank you, April Dawn and CAFE, for this unique, creative feedback."

## Valérie Sky - Spring 2024

**ENG 2200** *Introduction to English Linguistics*College of Letters, Arts, and Social Sciences

faculty reflection

### Valérie Sky

×

"In the Spring 2024 semester, April came to observe my ENG 2200 Introduction to English Linguistics class to perform the Faculty Consultation Method Based on Still Photography in the Classroom. At first, I was a little nervous: Being observed is one thing, but being photographed while teaching is different. Would the students agree to appear in pictures? Will they feel tense and “act” rather than learn? What would we all look like in the pictures?... Serious? Bored? Impatient? Distracted? Tired? Theatrically excited?

But as the class unfolded, I forgot the camera was even there, as April had a way of blending into the background, seamlessly moving through the room like she had always belonged there. Having her there gave me a chance to step outside myself and witness my work literally from a third-person perspective, the angle I had only read in writing but not seen before. However, when people write their narrative about your class after a peer observation, there is always a chance for that sly impostor syndrome to creep in, saying, “They are just being too nice.” It is much harder to argue with the hard evidence that pictures present. I remember that during the meeting when we reviewed the pictures, April said, “In this photo, the students are talking, engaged, and smiling,” and I nodded to agree. That day, the impostor syndrome, who is usually quick to speak, had nothing to add."

Close

## Outcomes and Impacts

### **Consultation Benefits**

Faculty members receive valuable feedback on teaching methods and student engagement through photographic observations.

### **Positive Feedback**

Professors have expressed appreciation for the insights gained from the project, highlighting its positive impact on their teaching.

### **Process Overview**

April Dawn visits your classroom at individual faculty request, provides photo releases for all participants, captures photographs, and discusses her observations with faculty to reveal insights about student learning and classroom dynamics.

### **Diverse Perspectives**

Feedback from various faculty members emphasizes the project's role in enhancing teaching effectiveness and fostering active learning environments.

### **Emotional Impact**

Educators have reported that viewing the photographs evokes powerful emotional responses, helping them reflect on their teaching experiences and student interactions.
