### Liza Taylor

Political Science
learn more

### Liza Taylor

![](img/new-faculty-2022/alicetaylor.jpg)

Political Science

#### Education

* PhD Political Science, UCLA
* MSc Political Theory, LSE
* MSc Gender, LSE
* BA Philosophy and Political Science with minor in Women's Studies, Union College

#### Research Interests

Attentive to the politics of citation that, as Sara Ahmed has argued, tells us that one “becomes a theorist by citing other theorists that cite theorists,” my research goals are oriented toward a politics of subversive citation that might urge contemporary political theorists in the direction of appreciating the sophisticated conceptual renderings of U.S. Black and Women of Color feminism. Situated in relation to the resist platforms emerging in the wake of the 2016 presidential election, my forthcoming book (Duke University Press), Feminism in Coalition: Thinking with U.S. Women of Color Feminism, presents early U.S. Women of Color coalitional feminism as an indispensable resource to contemporary political theory, feminist studies, and intersectional social justice activism. My second single-authored book project (currently in progress) turns again to U.S. Women of Color feminists to revisit the controversy surrounding the political cul-de-sac of deconstructive feminism emerging in the 1980s and 1990s. By contextualizing deconstructive feminism in relation to U.S. Women of Color feminism (which predates deconstructive feminism by almost two decades), I seek to set the stage for an imagined site of generative feminist exchange that might help us to avoid the pitfalls of deconstructive feminism’s most vexing commitments. Finally, in a co-edited book project, colleagues and I are exploring the role “pedagogies of discomfort” might play in political science classrooms oriented toward an “equity,” as opposed to a “diversity,” framework. Centering critical inquiry, a normative commitment to social justice, and the move to “action,” an equity approach calls into question more traditional approaches to political science. In this edited volume, we seek to not only make the case for why an equity frame is needed, but we also plan to offer a range of practical “how-to” guides on incorporating an equity focus and balancing liberatory pedagogies of both love and discomfort in the political science classroom.

### Tierra Ellis

![](img/new-faculty-2022/ttellis.jpg)

Psychology

#### Education

* PhD School Psychology, Howard University
* BA Psychology, Arizona State University
* BA African and African American History

#### Research Interests

Dr. Tierra T. Ellis received her doctorate in school psychology from Howard University, with an emphasis in forensic psychology. She completed her APA-Accredited internship at the Illinois School Psychology Internship Consortium (ISPIC) where she specialized in trauma at Egyptian Public and Mental Health Department (EHD), which was a dual clinical and school psychology focused site. She completed a 2-year APA accredited postdoctoral fellowship at Children's Hospital Los Angeles (CHLA) where she specialized in adolescent and young adult medicine. She is the Founder and Executive Director of Psyches of Color, Inc., a progressive nonprofit organization that uses strengths-based and culturally relevant approaches to provide mental health support to decrease the stigma of mental health and promote radical healing to at-promise Black and Latinx adolescents and young adults. She is a recent graduate of the Youth Justice Leadership Institute (YJLI) Fellowship within the National Juvenile Justice Network (NJJN). She is a Licensed Psychologist (LP), Nationally Certified School Psychologist (NCSP), and a member of Psychologists for Social Responsibility (Psy-SR) where she serves on the Abolition Working Group team. Dr. Tierra published a chapter in the book, Students with Parents Involved in Substance Abuse or Dependence (2020) and is currently completing a chapter in the book, Social Justice in Supervision and Field Experiences (in preparation). Her scholarship includes dismantling the school-to-prison pipeline, destigmatizing mental health in Black and Latinx communities, culturally relevant interventions for at-promise Black and Latinx adolescents and young adults, and the representation of Black students in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Math (STEM). Dr. Tierra conducts Hip Hop and Healing workshops as she is super passionate about creating and integrating culturally relevant approaches such as Hip-Hop culture and psychotherapeutic treatments for which she is committed to expanding in the mental health field.

### Liza Taylor

Political Science
learn more

### Liza Taylor

![](img/new-faculty-2022/alicetaylor.jpg)

Political Science

#### Education

* PhD Political Science, UCLA
* MSc Political Theory, LSE
* MSc Gender, LSE
* BA Philosophy and Political Science with minor in Women's Studies, Union College

#### Research Interests

Attentive to the politics of citation that, as Sara Ahmed has argued, tells us that one “becomes a theorist by citing other theorists that cite theorists,” my research goals are oriented toward a politics of subversive citation that might urge contemporary political theorists in the direction of appreciating the sophisticated conceptual renderings of U.S. Black and Women of Color feminism. Situated in relation to the resist platforms emerging in the wake of the 2016 presidential election, my forthcoming book (Duke University Press), Feminism in Coalition: Thinking with U.S. Women of Color Feminism, presents early U.S. Women of Color coalitional feminism as an indispensable resource to contemporary political theory, feminist studies, and intersectional social justice activism. My second single-authored book project (currently in progress) turns again to U.S. Women of Color feminists to revisit the controversy surrounding the political cul-de-sac of deconstructive feminism emerging in the 1980s and 1990s. By contextualizing deconstructive feminism in relation to U.S. Women of Color feminism (which predates deconstructive feminism by almost two decades), I seek to set the stage for an imagined site of generative feminist exchange that might help us to avoid the pitfalls of deconstructive feminism’s most vexing commitments. Finally, in a co-edited book project, colleagues and I are exploring the role “pedagogies of discomfort” might play in political science classrooms oriented toward an “equity,” as opposed to a “diversity,” framework. Centering critical inquiry, a normative commitment to social justice, and the move to “action,” an equity approach calls into question more traditional approaches to political science. In this edited volume, we seek to not only make the case for why an equity frame is needed, but we also plan to offer a range of practical “how-to” guides on incorporating an equity focus and balancing liberatory pedagogies of both love and discomfort in the political science classroom.
