## **What advice would you share with our first-generation college students?**

They matter and have a contribution to make to anywhere they go in life. They should ask questions and participate in events and campus experiences that will contribute to their growth and development. If they have a goal of going to graduate school, they should do it. Find someone on-campus that will guide and support you with your post-graduation plans.

## **What was your experience as a first-generation college student?**

My college experience as a first-generation college student was overwhelming. I couldn't go to my parents because they didn't understand the commitment required, financial aid jargon, and the need to spend hours studying. I had to adjust my study habits based on what I had going on in my personal life and learn how to navigate college on my own and with the help of friends. My college experience made me realize what I wanted to do after graduation. I decided I wanted to be a role model and mentor for other students to help them achieve their own educational goals.

## **What advice would you share with our first-generation college students?**

Get involved! Take advantage of all the resources available through student advocacy and programming. Ask questions and be critical of information that they may receive. Most importantly try new things, and step out of comfort to gain better understanding of themselves and the world around them.

## **What was your experience as a first-generation college student?**

I did not really understand the academic institution/system. I took classes not needed for my degree and did not understand the requirements or importance of attending office hours and developing relationships with faculty. I also struggled balancing familial demands, school, understanding myself, my needs, and time management overall. Although I had an older brother at CPP with me, we were both just as lost. We had to figure it out on our own without help from our parents because they were not familiar with how to navigate through the institution.

As an undergraduate student I became heavily involved through my years with the [Cultural Centers](https://www.cpp.edu/~oslcc/), the undocumented club on campus, the Multicultural Council, [ASI Student Government](https://asi.cpp.edu/student-government/), [Orientation Services](https://www.cpp.edu/~orientation/index.shtml),[PolyTransfer](https://www.cpp.edu/~polytransfer/), and the[Undocumented Services](https://www.cpp.edu/~deanofstudents/bdrc/) on campus. Being involved helped me find resources and support from faculty and staff to reach beyond my own limits. If I had not tapped into my community for help in navigating through the academic institution, I would not be where I am today.

## **What advice would you share with our first-generation college students?**

Give yourself time to learn who you are. Set high, but attainable standards, and measure your success by those standards. Allow yourself the flexibility to grow and change and want new things in your life. Make temporary sacrifices when necessary for long-term success. You never know what the path you are paving today will lead to, so say yes to every opportunity, and never regret an experience.

## **What was your experience as a first-generation college student?**

I feel a lot of pressure in my role as a first-generation college student to excel in life and lead by example. My family couldn't help me and my parents were not at all involved in the process of applying for schools or choosing my major. I enjoyed college but I had to rely on myself to set my goals and stay motivated to achieve them. I utilized resources at school such as the career center, [federal work study](https://www.cpp.edu/financial-aid/federal-work-study/index.shtml), and teachers' office hours in order to excel. I experienced so much satisfaction at graduation, knowing I had invested six years of my life in pursuit of that moment.

Get connected with people who achieved goals that are similar to yours. Learn from their experience. Get their advice. Get recommendations and referrals to resources.

I worked hard and did well academically, but nobody was available in my personal life to offer advice on career options, resources, and opportunities. Eventually I learned earning good grades is not enough. Academic success must be supplemented by meeting the right people combined with involvement in the right activities.

## **What advice would you share with our first-generation college students?**

Three things: 1. You're not alone, 2. It's ok to ask for help, and 3. Learn from your mistakes (because they will happen).

## **What was your experience as a first-generation college student?**

I was always afraid that others would see me as not good enough for college, so I tried my best to blend in and not draw attention. I realized that I needed to invest in my education not only for myself but for people like who my parents who sacrificed so much to get me to that point.

## **What was your experience as a first-generation college student?**

My parents always encouraged me to go beyond high school but could offer me little insight into college success. I often sat in classes silent, paralyzed and unable to utter answers to questions posed for fear of my ignorance being revealed. After my first year, I seriously considered dropping out because of feelings of inadequacy and cultural alienation. I was one of a handful of African American students. But I learned the adaptive skills--I mimicked more successful students and their study patterns. I worked collaboratively with others who were better prepared. I sought out faculty mentors that saw my potential and worked tirelessly with me. I wrote down prepared questions and answers to limit my anxiety in class discussions. I began to seek out courses of study that gave me a sense of identity and purpose. I was empowered to learn that people who came from similar backgrounds were often behind in academic skills because they were born into situations of impoverished schools and neighborhoods. Moreover, these gaps in performance came as a consequence of historical trends and discriminatory policies that limited their options and often diminished their dreams. By my junior and senior years, the students who refused to work with me because of my perceived inferiority, now courted by opinion and sought me out as a study partner. Eventually, I graduated with honors. It was a long path with many obstacles but I have reached the point where I can now help others avoid the many pitfalls of being a first-generation college student.

## **What advice would you share with our first-generation college students?**

You are not alone. College is new and can sometimes be overwhelming; if you don’t know something, it's okay to ask questions. Try new things such as meeting others from different backgrounds or join various clubs and organizations. Visiting your professors during office hours is a must -- this helps them to get to know you better. Apply for scholarships; there are so many funds that go unclaimed. Look for opportunities to work on campus because it is convenient and offices will work around your student schedule. Find opportunties to go study abroad as it will help you see the world and broaden your horizons.

## **What was your experience as a first-generation college student?**

It was scary for me because I felt intimated. I was afraid I would get lost, wouldn't make any friends, or other students wouldn't be friendly with me. I didn't do as well my first year because I did not have good study habits, although I had always gotten high grades from K-12th grade. College work was different and much more demanding than I was used to. However, I learned to “swim” and got connected with resources and services on campus. Once I found my community and support system, I felt like I belonged.
